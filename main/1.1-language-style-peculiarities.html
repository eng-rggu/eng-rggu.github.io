1.1 Language Style Peculiarities

Содержание > Unit 1 > Chapter 1


     It is common knowledge that written language varies in style. Some lexical and grammatical features contribute to variation in style appropriate for specific topic, purpose and audience.
    Prior to attempting any piece of written translation, the translator should follow some general rules to achieve adequate results. First of all, the translator should determine the language style of the original text which is to be preserved in his translation.  As the stylistic colouring of any original text depends on the form of writing, the purpose of writing and the particular audience the writer is addressing it is rather important for the interpreter to take into consideration these key points.
   According to the form of writing the original scientific or technical text may be:  a summary, a letter, a newspaper article, a diary, a leaflet, some notes, an advertisement, scientific research papers, a set of instructions, a review, some press releases, a brochure, etc.
     According to the purpose of writing the technical text may be: informing, persuading, describing, narrating, explaining, instructing, analysing or comparing.
     Writing has a communicative purpose.  Any technical text is designed for some audience. The writer usually has an idea who his target audience is going to be and changes or adjusts his style accordingly. The writer may do this intuitively. In case he is asked to write a simple story for young children he would – subconsciously – use short sentences and plain words. But if his audience is more sophisticated, he needs to adjust his writing style too.
    Language modification is an essential part of the writing process. Aimed at preserving the stylistic colouring of the target language (TL)* the translator should choose the adequate vocabulary in his mother tongue (MT)* in order to do the translation in the same lexicon.


*Target language (TL) – English; Mother tongue (MT) – Russian.

Study the chart given below. Here are some examples of how different writing purpose affects the style and content of writing.

WRITING   PURPOSES                     WRITING   FEATURES


      INFORMING                             - Clear introduction.
                                                          - Straightforward sentences and paragraph
                                                            structure.          
                                                          - Use of facts and figures.
                                                          - Factual details.

    1. Example: “A computer is a machine with an intricate network of electronic circuits that operate switches or magnetize tiny metal cores.”


     PERSUADING                           - Colourful and descriptive language.
                                                          - A personal style.

     2. Example: “A computer is one of the smartest machines of the new generation. Take your time to explore its endless possibilities...”  


     DESCRIBING                          -Longer sentences.
                                                         - Use of adjectives and comparisons – similes
                                                          and metaphors.
                                                         - An organizational move from general  
                                                          description to the specific.

     3. Example: “Some advanced scientific research has given rise to principally new computers.”


    NARRATING                              - The story structure.
                                                          - A chronological order.
                                                          - An introduction.
                                                          - Use of adverbs and phrases to describe how
                                                           things happened.

     4. Example: “Things started to fall into place. The silence and the monotony of the scientific laboratory were interrupted by the appearance of an intelligent and ambitious young man, named Bill. He worked tirelessly and was bound to succeed. He revolutionized the development of the new generation of the computers.”

   EXPLAINING                              - Carefully structured information that can be                                                                      
                                                           traced back to its original source.
                                                         - Definition of technical terms and the use of
                                                           technical language.
                                                         - Factual description.
                                                         - Concise formal structure – possibly
                                                            measured in some way.

     5. Example: “A computer cannot do anything unless a person tells it what to do and uploads it with the appropriate information; but because the electric pulses can move at the speed of light, a computer can carry out a great number of arithmetic and logical operations almost instantaneously.”



   INSTRUCTING                            - A stepped structure with a series of mini-
                                                             tasks.
                                                           - Use of imperatives: “Do this...” to instruct the
                                                             reader what to do.
                                                           - Short, clear sentences.
                                                           - Factual description to identify information,
                                                              rather than describe it.

     6. Example: “Use HELP frequently – it will expedite your learning process and minimize unnecessary trips to the Framework manuals”.


   ANALYSING                                - A paragraphed structure in which each
                                                             paragraph deals with a particular aspect.
                                                          - References to what is being analysed, either
                                                             in the form of a summary,
                                                             quotation (the words of the original) or
                                                             anecdote (your version of something in the
                                                             original).
                                                           - Longer sentences to cope with the
                                                             complexity of the analysis.          

     7. Example: “Computer programs vary greatly in purpose, style, and complexity. Nearly all programs, however, go through the same process consisting of three phases:
          1)  describing, collecting, and storing information;
          2)  processing the data to achieve the desired result;
          3) displaying and / or storing the results;”
    
      
     COMPARING                             - A two-sided structure where ideas or facts
                                                            are set against one another.
                                                          - Complex sentences, using a wide range of
                                                            connectives and connecting phrases:
                                                           “nevertheless”, “on the other hand”,
                                                           “but”, “in contrast”...

     8. Example: “In 1960, the second generation of computers was developed and they could perform tasks ten times faster than their predecessors. Second-generation computers were smaller, faster and more dependable than first-generation computers.”
